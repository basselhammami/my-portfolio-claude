CLM Platform — Designing for High Stakes

Led the end-to-end design of RAKBANK's Customer Lifecycle Management platform, Decision Engine tool, and a broader suite of internal tools.

01 — CONTEXT

The Context

RAKBANK is one of the UAE's largest retail and business banks, serving over 900,000 customers. The Customer Lifecycle Management (CLM) platform is the internal tool used by compliance and KYC teams to manage customer onboarding, periodic reviews, and regulatory reporting.

When I joined, the platform was a patchwork of legacy screens, inconsistent patterns, and workflows that had grown organically over years. Staff were navigating between multiple tools to complete a single task.

02 — USERS

What Was Broken

Three systemic problems were identified through stakeholder interviews and workflow observation.

01

Fragmented information architecture

Customer data was split across 6+ screens with no unified view. Analysts had to open multiple tabs to see a complete customer picture.

02

Inconsistent interaction patterns

Similar actions had different UI treatments across modules. Staff had to relearn workflows when switching between tasks.

03

No workflow visibility

There was no way to see where a case was in the review process, who had touched it, or what was blocking it.

03 — PROBLEM

Who Uses CLM

The platform serves three primary user groups within RAKBANK's compliance function.

KYC Analysts

Front-line staff who complete customer due diligence, verify documents, and flag risk indicators.

Compliance Officers

Senior reviewers who approve high-risk cases, manage escalations, and ensure regulatory adherence.

Operations Managers

Team leads who monitor workload distribution, track SLA compliance, and report to senior management.

"I spend 40% of my time navigating between screens just to get the information I need to make a decision."

KYC Analyst, RAKBANK

08 — DESIGN DECISIONS

Key Design Decisions

Four decisions that defined the platform's design direction.

1

Single-page profile over tabbed navigation

Tabs hide context. Analysts need to cross-reference data constantly — a single scrollable page with sticky section headers was faster in testing.

2

Role-based dashboard views

Analysts and managers have fundamentally different information needs. Separate default views with shared underlying data.

3

Inline document preview

Opening documents in a separate viewer broke the review flow. An inline panel kept analysts in context.

4

Progressive disclosure for complex cases

Standard cases show a simplified view. High-risk or complex cases surface additional fields and context automatically.

10 — REFLECTION

What I Learned

1

Compliance UX requires a different mental model

The goal is not to make things fast — it's to make correctness achievable. Speed is a byproduct of removing friction, not the primary objective.

2

Information architecture is the hardest problem

Getting the data model right before designing screens saved weeks of rework. The profile structure went through 8 iterations before we found the right grouping.

3

Trust is a design output

Analysts didn't trust the old system's data. Building visible data provenance — showing where each piece of information came from — was more impactful than any UI improvement.
